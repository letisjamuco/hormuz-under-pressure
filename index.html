The boarding pass on your screen is missing two pieces of information.

You don't know where the flight departs from, and you don't know where it lands. The fare is also blank. That is not an accident. The aviation system rarely tells passengers why a ticket costs what it costs, and almost never points to the upstream events that set the price in motion.

This project fills in the missing fields. It traces a single shockwave: a geopolitical disruption around the Strait of Hormuz, a narrow body of water between Oman and Iran through which roughly a quarter of the world's seaborne oil passes. When tension builds there, oil prices climb within days. Jet fuel, the largest cost item for most airlines, follows. Surcharges appear. Base fares drift upward. Some routes are quietly rerouted to avoid closed airspace. Eventually, weeks later, the cost arrives at the gate where you board.

en route to chapter 01
CH·01

The chokepoint

A narrow strait. A quarter of the world's oil. No real backup route.

The Strait of Hormuz is just 33 km wide at its narrowest point. Tankers pass single-file through shipping lanes flanked by Iranian territorial waters on one side and Omani waters on the other.

Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait, the UAE, and Qatar export almost all of their oil through it. Only Saudi Arabia and the UAE operate pipelines that can partially bypass the strait, with limited capacity.

  • Tehran has threatened closure repeatedly. Closure has never been completed.
  • In April 2026, the strait operates under heavy restrictions.
  • Most oil leaving Hormuz heads to Asia (China, India, Japan, South Korea).

Data source: EIA · IEA

The oil shock

Brent crude responds in days. Jet fuel follows within weeks.

When markets price in a Hormuz disruption, Brent crude moves first. Jet fuel, refined from crude, lags by a few weeks but tracks closely. The chart on the right overlays the Kaggle scenario series (2019 to 2026) with FRED's real Brent series for comparison.

Dotted vertical lines mark conflict events from our event table. Months when the strait was disrupted are shaded.

Data source: Kaggle · FRED · EIA

The airline response

Some airlines pass the cost to you. Others absorb it. Most do both, partially.

Once jet fuel costs rise, airlines have a choice: pass the cost to passengers, absorb it through margin, or hedge with fuel contracts signed in advance. The two views below compare the airline response from different angles.

First, the small multiples show each airline as its own mini-story. Then, the slope view compares how prices move from the baseline period into the shock period.

Data source: Kaggle Airline Ticket Prices vs Oil & Fuel Costs
